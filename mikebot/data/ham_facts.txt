Amateur radio operators are licensed by the FCC in the US across three license classes: Technician, General, and Amateur Extra.
The first transatlantic radio transmission was made by Guglielmo Marconi on December 12, 1901.
Ham radio operators provided critical communications after Hurricane Katrina when commercial infrastructure failed.
The ARRL (American Radio Relay League) was founded in 1914 and is the largest amateur radio organization in the US.
Ham radio is licensed in virtually every country in the world, with over 3 million operators globally.
The phonetic alphabet used in ham radio - Alpha Bravo Charlie - was standardized by NATO in 1956.
CQ is the general call used to invite any station to respond. It comes from the French word securite.
73 means best regards in ham radio. It originated from the Phillips Code used by telegraph operators in the 1800s.
88 means love and kisses in ham radio. It is traditionally sent at the end of a conversation with a close friend.
The first amateur radio satellite, OSCAR 1, was launched on December 12, 1961 - just 4 years after Sputnik.
Morse code was invented by Samuel Morse and Alfred Vail in the 1830s and is still used by ham operators today.
The 2-meter band (144-148 MHz) is the most popular VHF amateur band for local and regional communications.
A Maidenhead grid square locator divides the world into a grid system used universally by ham operators.
The W1AW station at ARRL headquarters in Newington, CT has been operating since 1938.
Amateur radio operators were first called hams as a derogatory term by professional telegraph operators - then it was adopted proudly.
HF (high frequency) bands from 3-30 MHz can bounce signals off the ionosphere to reach thousands of miles.
The K-index measures geomagnetic storm intensity. High K-index means poor HF propagation but possible aurora.
Solar flux index (SFI) measures solar radio emissions at 10.7cm. Higher SFI means better HF propagation.
Elmer is the affectionate ham radio term for an experienced operator who mentors newcomers.
The minimum age to get a ham radio license in the US is any age - there is no minimum age requirement.
Field Day is held on the last full weekend of June each year and is the most popular ham radio event in North America.
The International Space Station has amateur radio equipment aboard and astronauts regularly contact schools.
APRS (Automatic Packet Reporting System) uses packet radio to transmit GPS position and other data.
Echolink connects amateur radio stations over the internet, allowing worldwide contacts via VoIP.
Winlink is a global email system for ham operators that works over HF, VHF, and satellite links.
The fox hunt is a popular amateur radio activity where participants use direction-finding equipment to locate a hidden transmitter.
Amateur Extra class licensees have access to all amateur radio bands and privileges in the US.
The Technician license is the entry-level US ham license and grants full VHF/UHF privileges.
A repeater receives a signal on one frequency and retransmits it on another, extending communication range.
The term rubber duck refers to the short flexible antenna commonly used with handheld radios.
QRP operation means running low power - typically 5 watts or less. QRP operators pride themselves on long-distance contacts with minimal power.
The ionosphere has multiple layers (D, E, F1, F2) that reflect radio waves back to Earth for long-distance communication.
Skywarn is a network of trained ham radio operators who report severe weather to the National Weather Service.
The hobby of amateur radio includes CW (Morse), phone (voice), digital modes, satellite, moonbounce (EME), and more.
Moonbounce or EME (Earth-Moon-Earth) involves bouncing radio signals off the Moon's surface to communicate.
The POTA (Parks on the Air) program encourages ham operators to operate from national and state parks.
A beam antenna (Yagi) focuses radio energy in one direction for stronger signals than an omnidirectional antenna.
The longest confirmed QSO (contact) distance is Earth to the Moon and back - about 800,000 km round trip.
DX means distance in ham radio. Working DX means making contacts with stations far away or in rare locations.
The Most Wanted DXCC list tracks rare entities that few hams have confirmed. Bouvet Island is often #1.
A contest in ham radio involves making as many contacts as possible within a set time period for points.
The DXCC (DX Century Club) award requires confirmed contacts with 100 different DXCC entities worldwide.
Ham radio played a key role in coordinating relief efforts after the 2011 Tohoku earthquake in Japan.
The CW (continuous wave) Morse code mode can copy signals 10-15 dB weaker than voice modes.
Every licensed amateur radio operator in the US has their callsign, name, and address in a public FCC database.
The ham radio community uses phonetic alphabets, Q-codes, and RST reports (readability, strength, tone) for standardized communication.
DMR (Digital Mobile Radio) is a popular digital voice mode used on VHF/UHF repeaters and simplex channels.
FT8 is a digital weak-signal mode developed by Joe Taylor K1JT that can make contacts with extremely low power.
The world record for the longest ham radio contact without repeater or internet assistance is over 22,000 km.
Handheld transceivers (HTs) like the Baofeng UV-5R have introduced millions of new operators to amateur radio.
The term YL means young lady and refers to any female operator regardless of age. OM means old man for male operators.
